Built a fintech at 16. 10,000 users. Shut down by regulators.

The startup was called Crito.

I was 16. I'd been running digital marketing campaigns since I was 14. I knew how to get users. I knew how to grow something. What I didn't know was how to build a company that regulators couldn't touch.

We hit 10,000 users. Real people, using the product every day.

Then it ended. Not because the product was broken. Not because we ran out of money. Because we'd built fast and told ourselves compliance was a later-stage problem.

That decision cost us everything.

The hardest part wasn't losing the startup. It was knowing 10,000 people had trusted us with something real, and we couldn't protect what we'd built for them.

The shutdown had nothing to do with effort. Nothing to do with the product. It came down to a category of risk we'd parked as someone else's future problem.

Most first-time founders do this. Compliance feels like a constraint. Something for lawyers. Something you bolt on when you have revenue and runway and time. So you push it.

That's the trap.

When I started building Sbl.so, I built it differently. Regulatory and compliance considerations went into the structure early. Not because I suddenly enjoy paperwork. Because I'd already paid what it costs to treat them as optional.

Moving fast matters. I still believe that. But moving fast into the wrong structure just means you hit the wall sooner.

Crito taught me that. Still does.
