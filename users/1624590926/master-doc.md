## YOUR STORY

I didn't come from a glamorous tech background. I started my journey
with a **Diploma in Computer Science**, where most people around me were
just focused on finishing the course and getting any job possible.

But I got curious about how software actually works.

During diploma, I started experimenting with small coding projects ---
basic apps, simple automation scripts, and eventually Python. That
curiosity slowly pulled me into the world of **data and machine
learning**.

After my diploma, I pursued **B.Tech in Computer Science**. That's where
things got serious.

I realized something quickly:

Most students were studying just for exams.

But companies wanted **skills and projects**.

So I started learning outside the syllabus --- Python, data science, ML
libraries, GitHub projects, online courses, and building small models
just to understand how things worked.

It wasn't smooth.

I remember spending nights debugging code that refused to run, tutorials
that didn't explain enough, and moments where I felt like everyone else
understood things faster.

But slowly things started clicking.

Projects improved. Confidence improved. Interviews became easier.

Eventually, I landed a role as a **Machine Learning Engineer at
Cognizant**.

Now I work on building and deploying ML models, solving real business
problems with data, and constantly learning how AI is evolving.

And along the way, I share what I learn --- so other engineers don't
have to struggle the same way I did.

## CORE OPINIONS & ANGLES

### Most ML tutorials don't prepare you for real ML work

Online tutorials show clean datasets and perfect workflows. Real-world
ML is messy --- bad data, unclear problems, and business constraints.

### Projects matter more than certificates

Companies rarely care about how many courses you completed. They care
about what you **built, shipped, and solved**.

### AI engineers need software skills

Many ML engineers struggle because they focus only on models. Real AI
systems require **software engineering, deployment, and data
pipelines**.

### Learning in public accelerates growth

When you share what you learn, you're forced to understand it deeply.
Teaching is one of the fastest ways to master technical skills.
